**System:** UMA Universal

---

## **1. Purpose**
The TPS Lifecycle defines the **full sequence of states** a module passes through when being anchored using the Two‑Pin System.  
It ensures that pins evolve safely, predictably, and in alignment with:

- stability  
- pacing  
- accessibility  
- render integrity  
- cognitive load  
- SIAP governance  

This lifecycle applies to all modules capable of canonical anchoring.

---

## **2. Lifecycle Overview**
A TPS pin moves through **seven stages**:

1. **Proposal**  
2. **Pre‑Check**  
3. **SIAP Evaluation**  
4. **Pairing**  
5. **Veto or Approval**  
6. **Activation**  
7. **Revision or Retirement**

Each stage has strict requirements to prevent premature anchoring or cosmic‑layer collapse.

---

## **3. Lifecycle Stages**

---

### **3.1 Stage 1 — Proposal**  
A module becomes a candidate for anchoring when:

- its conceptual scope is complete  
- its role in the architecture is defined  
- its dependencies are known  
- its boundaries are stable  

The proposal may originate from:

- architecture development  
- safety review  
- navigation requirements  
- cosmic‑layer planning  

**Output:** Module enters TPS consideration.

---

### **3.2 Stage 2 — Pre‑Check**  
Before SIAP evaluation, the module undergoes a preliminary check for:

- conceptual completeness  
- render stability  
- viewport behavior  
- dependency readiness  
- cognitive load safety  

If the module fails pre‑check, it cannot proceed.

**Output:** Module is either cleared for SIAP or held.

---

### **3.3 Stage 3 — SIAP Evaluation**  
SIAP evaluates the module against:

- stability requirements  
- accessibility requirements  
- pacing requirements  
- cognitive load requirements  
- render integrity requirements  
- governance requirements  

SIAP may request clarification or additional structure.

**Output:** SIAP issues a **Pass** or **Fail**.

---

### **3.4 Stage 4 — Pairing**  
If SIAP passes the module:

- a **Map Pin** is created in the module’s directory  
- a **SIAP Pin** is created in the SIAP ledger  
- each pin references the other  
- the module enters the dual‑anchor state  

If SIAP fails the module, pairing does not occur.

**Output:** Module becomes “paired” or “vetoed.”

---

### **3.5 Stage 5 — Veto or Approval**  
SIAP may:

- **Approve** the pin (module is ready for anchoring)  
- **Veto** the pin (module is not ready)  

Reasons for veto include:

- premature cosmic anchoring  
- viewport collapse  
- ring bunching  
- pacing misalignment  
- dependency instability  
- cognitive overload  

Example: **CP‑00 Cosmic Architecture Pin vetoed.**

**Output:** Pin is either approved or vetoed.

---

### **3.6 Stage 6 — Activation**  
A pin becomes **active** only when:

- both pins exist  
- SIAP approves alignment  
- the module is stable across contexts  
- the module is ready for dependency anchoring  

Activation grants the module:

- canonical status  
- architectural stability  
- navigational reliability  
- governance protection  

Example: **AP‑01 Astrolabe Pin activated.**

**Output:** Module becomes fully anchored.

---

### **3.7 Stage 7 — Revision or Retirement**  
Over time, a module may require:

- structural updates  
- accessibility improvements  
- pacing adjustments  
- render fixes  
- conceptual expansion  

If changes are minor:

- the Map Pin is updated  
- the SIAP Pin logs the revision  

If changes are major:

- the pin may be retired  
- a new pin may be created  
- SIAP logs the transition  

**Output:** Module remains stable across evolution.

---
